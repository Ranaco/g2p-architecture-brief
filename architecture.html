Government-to-Person Disbursement · System Architecture

A built, verifiable anti-corruption ledger — not a diagram.

A working proof-of-concept where a government payment travels treasury→citizen with every step signed by an independent institution, forgery rejected, and the record impossible for anyone — including the government — to rewrite. Money stays on bank rails; the blockchain holds only the proof.

Non-custodial · records, not money 4 independent validators · Besu QBFT Bitcoin-anchored ~170 tests · 5 adversarial reviews
The architecture · one picture

Four layers, one direction of flow

A payment succeeds on a local Postgres commit; the chain is a downstream witness. That separation is what lets it scale and stay robust.

The trust model, in one line Independent validators mean no single institution can rewrite a record. The Bitcoin anchor means not even all four together can back-date one. Everything on-chain is a hash or salted commitment — never PII, never money.

How this differs from the original sketch

Signing was never the gap — consensus custody was

The reference design had the right thesis (records-not-money, off the critical path, HMAC commitments, Bitcoin anchor) and genuine multi-party signing — the finance ministry independently signed the funds step, banks signed outcomes. We kept all of that. The difference is a precise one:

DimensionReference sketchThis build
SigningIndependent signers already (Finance signs funds, banks sign outcomes)Kept — same multi-party signing
Validator custodyConceded its validators sat under one operator ("little edge over a WORM DB"); real distribution deferred to "Phase 2"Distributed on day one — 4 independent institutions incl. an Audit Court; 3-of-4 to commit
Rewrite resistanceSigning doesn't stop the node operator from rewriting the ledgerNo operator can rewrite — consensus + Bitcoin anchor
MaturityIllustrative sketch — sample data, in-browser toyRunning, tested system — ~170 tests, live end-to-end
Last-mile security"signed callbacks" as a conceptECDSA non-repudiation; forgery + cross-bank hijack rejected
Reliability"off the critical path" as a principleProven by an automated chaos test
The crux Rewriting a ledger is a validator (consensus) power, not a signer power — so multiple independent signers on a chain one operator secures is still rewritable by that operator. The reference conceded this; we fixed it by distributing validator custody, and made the whole system real.
Said plainly

Honest limits

This is a proof-of-concept Throwaway dev keys, single-machine Docker, simulated banks. The Bitcoin anchor is real but confirms in hours (calendar-level immediately, upgrades to a block attestation later). The chain proves records are unaltered and attributable — it does not catch ghost/duplicate citizens (beaten upstream by identity/biometrics) and, without citizen confirmation, proves settlement to an account, not cash-in-hand. Production needs real independent validator custody, HSM key management, an independent audit, and a signed DPIA.
