G2P Fund-Traceability · Architecture Brief

Prove where every dollar went — without a blockchain ever touching the money.

A government pays 10,000 citizens $1 each through commercial banks. The goal is not to move that money on-chain — it is to make the record of it impossible for any single institution to forge or rewrite. Here is the simplest architecture that does that, and the one decision it hinges on.

Non-custodial · records, not money Chain off the critical path Zero Central-Bank APIs
The one-line answer

A boring Postgres ledger is the source of truth; a managed 4-validator permissioned chain, each node held by a different institution, witnesses every step; a nightly hash is anchored to Bitcoin so not even the consortium can back-date it. Money never leaves the banking rails.

How it works · six tiers, one direction of flow

The architecture

Each tier has one job. A payment succeeds the instant the database commits — everything below the line is a subscriber that can lag, backlog, or go dark without ever freezing a disbursement.

tier 1Money
Moves on existing bank / RTGS / ACH rails. The platform never holds or transmits a cent. No tokenised currency — that would trigger e-money regulation.
▼ one local transaction ▼
tier 2Truth
One append-only Postgres. Payment lines + an outbox row written in the same transaction. This commit — nothing else — is what makes a payment real.
tier 3Control
Maker → checker → authorizer, plus inter-institutional co-signatures, enforced in the app workflow. Money is not instructed until the required signatures exist. This is the real gate.
▼ async · off the critical path ▼
tier 4Witness
A managed permissioned chain (Besu/QBFT via Kaleido or SettleMint). Four validators, one each held by Finance Ministry · Central Bank · a third body · an independent NGO. A relayer drains the outbox. One thin contract records signed status transitions.
tier 5Anchor
Nightly Merkle root → Bitcoin (OpenTimestamps). This is what makes “even the consortium cannot rewrite history” true — forging it would mean rewriting Bitcoin.
tier 6Reporting
Dashboards read Postgres projections, never the chain — so reporting is always fast and always up, whatever the chain is doing.
What changed · the architectural shift

From a single authority to independent custody

The original sketch and this design share the same skeleton — non-custodial, records-not-money, chain off the critical path. They differ on the one thing that decides whether a blockchain earns its keep at all: who holds the validators, and who is allowed to authorize a step.

Previous · single authority New · independent consortium
Who runs the nodes
One entity — the executing agency holds operator, verifier and authorizer, and every validator runs under one admin.
Four distinct legal custodians — Finance Ministry, Central Bank, a third body, and an independent NGO — one node each.
Where trust comes from
You still have to trust the operator’s copy; the nightly public anchor is the only external check.
No single operator sits in the trust base — trust rests on independent consensus plus a mandatory outside signer.
The independent auditor / NGO
A read-only observer. It can verify the trail, but it gates nothing and signs nothing.
A validator and mandatory co-signer — nothing advances without it, and it signs against external bank evidence.
What a signature proves
That the operator’s own records are internally consistent and unaltered.
That separate institutions each attested, against bank-signed camt.053 settlement evidence.
The threat it actually stops
✕Accidental drift and outside tampering — not the operator itself.
✓Also the operator unilaterally rewriting or colluding — the real corruption threat.
The crux, in one line The previous design proves the record wasn’t altered. This one proves no single institution could have altered it. The original brief actually conceded the point — “3–4 independently-operated validators, or it shouldn’t be called a blockchain project” — but parked it as a Phase-2 aspiration. This design makes independence the day-one premise, because without it the chain is provably just a database.
The decision that trips everyone · block vs. witness

Approval gates the money — the chain only witnesses it

“Every regulator must approve before money moves” is the right control — but making that an on-chain gate is the trap. The only synchronous-gate precedent (Singapore’s Purpose-Bound Money) is custodial: it locks value inside the contract. That puts the chain on the critical path and pulls you under e-money law. So split it by volume:

Hard gate

Inter-institutional transfers

e.g. Central Bank → Ministry. A handful, high value. Require multi-signature (CB + Ministry + NGO), collected in the workflow before the instruction is released.

A slow NGO here delays one big transfer — tolerable.

Witness + flag

Mass citizen payouts

e.g. Ministry → banks → 10,000 people. Far too many to gate on a live signature. Banks sign outcomes, citizens confirm receipt, the NGO audits the aggregate after the fact.

A missing signature flags for investigation — it never freezes benefits.

What is written to the chain · and why so little

Proofs, never data

Regulators (EU’s EDPB, France’s CNIL) are explicit: an immutable ledger collides with the right to erasure. The resolution every serious project uses — put no personal data on-chain at all. Only commitments.

// on-chain — per payment, ~5 fields, immutable
{
  batchId,  itemId,  status,          // lifecycle state
  docHash,                          // SHA-256 of the exact instruction
  commit: HMAC(dni ‖ tenantSalt ‖ epochSalt), // keyed, salted, rotating
  signer,   timestamp
}

// off-chain — encrypted Postgres, erasable
{ name, dni, account, mobile, amount, geography, hmacSalt }
//                                          ↑ crypto-shred to "erase"
⚠ Exact, not hand-waved A plain hash of a national ID or phone number is brute-forceable in under a minute — a bare hash protects nothing. It must be a salted, keyed HMAC with rotating salts; deleting the salt is what performs erasure. Even then, regulators call this “close to compliant,” not true deletion — so a signed DPIA is a go-live requirement, not a nicety.
The last-mile problem · what makes an attestation real

Anchor bank-signed proof, not “we paid”

A hash proves a record is unaltered — not that money reached a person. So the NGO’s signature is only worth what it can independently see. Anchor bank-generated evidence, not the ministry’s own claim:

  • ▹ISO 20022 camt.053 / camt.054 — the receiving bank’s own statement that funds were booked to the beneficiary’s account, with machine-readable outcome codes (BOOK, Settlement Failed, RDFI NonSettlement). This distinguishes a real payout from a failed one.
  • ▹Beneficiary confirmation (SMS / USSD, or biometric cash-out for the unbanked) — the independent last-mile check that account-credit actually became cash-in-hand.
  • ▹The NGO signs against these external artifacts. Signing against the ministry’s internal record instead is a rubber stamp — it just co-signs the same story.
Why this exact shape · grounded in what shipped

The precedents — and where they fall short

World Bank FundsChain
2025 · Hyperledger Besu
Permissioned, non-custodial, only document hashes on-chain, off the critical path. Real scale: 13 projects / 10 countries → ~250 by June 2026. But single-owner today — the Bank runs all nodes; independent validators are only a roadmap item.
UN WFP Building Blocks
$555M · 25M transactions
Records, not money. Abandoned public Ethereum for permissioned. But WFP alone controls the chain — it can rewrite history; an official conceded the same result was achievable without any blockchain.
The lesson
why validators must be independent
A permissioned chain run by one entity is provably no better than an audited append-only database. Your design — independent validators from day one — is more ambitious on trust than either flagship. That is the whole point of it.
  • 62%of abandoned enterprise chains cite operational complexity as the cause — not the cryptography (Hyperledger, 2024). 87% of pilots never reach production.
  • ~10×Going from 1 to 4 independently-run nodes is roughly ten times harder, not four — per-org certificate authorities, key custody, cross-org governance.
  • →The simplicity lever that makes independence survivable: managed blockchain-as-a-service absorbs the PKI, HA, and monitoring. Four institutions hold keys; none runs the 3 a.m. machinery.
The one call that is yours, not the architecture’s

Is independent validator custody actually achievable?

Everything above is worth its cost only if the four validators sit under genuinely separate legal control. If they will realistically all be one agency, the blockchain adds nothing a database can’t. So:

✓ Yes — real custody

Build the consortium chain above

Central Bank + audit court/NGO each hold a key and run a managed node. You get a system genuinely stronger than FundsChain or WFP. Proceed exactly as specified.

✕ No — one operator

Drop the chain. Keep the anchor.

Use an audited append-only Postgres + the nightly Bitcoin anchor. Same real tamper-evidence, 40–60% less ops cost, none of the abandonment risk. The honest, simpler answer.
