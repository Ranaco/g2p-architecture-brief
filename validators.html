G2P Fund-Traceability · Validators & Signing Flow

Where the validators sit, and who signs at each step.

Two different jobs are easy to conflate. Getting them straight is the whole design: validators secure the ledger (who can write or rewrite it); signers authorize each business step (who vouches for it). Here is exactly how both work.

The distinction that matters

Validator ≠ signer

◆ Validator (consensus)

Who can write or rewrite the ledger. Four institutions each run a Besu QBFT node. 3 of 4 must agree to commit any record — so no single institution can insert or alter a payment. This applies to every step, identically.

✎ Signer (authorization)

Who vouches for a specific step. Each lifecycle step is signed with a particular institution's key (msg.sender). The set of required signers varies per step — and the smart contract rejects a step signed by the wrong party.

Why both are needed Multiple independent signers alone don't make a ledger tamper-proof — rewriting history is a validator power. A chain that one operator's validators secure is rewritable by that operator, no matter how many parties signed the individual steps. Independent validator custody is what makes "no one can rewrite the record" true.
1 · Where the validators sit

Four institutions, four nodes, 3-of-4 to commit

Each institution holds its own node and key under separate legal custody. The paying banks and the NGO/observer are NOT validators — banks are external signers (they sign payout outcomes); the NGO has read-only access with the power to raise on-chain disputes.

Any record is committed only when 3 of the 4 validators agree (QBFT). Survives 1 offline/malicious node. Immediate finality — no reorgs.

The two tiers, and the anchor

Gate the few, witness the many

Hard gates — the handful of high-value inter-institutional moments (fund release, final reconciliation) — require the signatures before proceeding. Witness — the thousands of citizen payouts — are recorded as they happen; a missing bank signature flags for investigation rather than freezing a nation's benefits. So an offline auditor can never halt payments, yet nothing escapes the record.

And above the four validators A nightly Merkle root of the reconciled ledger is stamped into Bitcoin (OpenTimestamps). So even if all four validators colluded, they couldn't back-date history without also rewriting Bitcoin. Independent validators stop one institution; the anchor stops all four together.
